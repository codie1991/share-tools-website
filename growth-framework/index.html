Junior & Intermediate Engineer Growth Reflection

Complete all five parts below. Your scores in Part 1 update the radar automatically. When you're done, generate a markdown summary to bring to your manager 1:1.

Part 1 Your Growth Radar Score yourself on all 8 dimensions

Use the 1–4 scale. Expand each dimension, pick your score — it updates the radar — then answer the two reflection prompts. Concrete examples beat long paragraphs.

Part 2 Patterns & Blockers Identify your consistency gaps and feedback patterns

Most engineers at Intermediate level have the capability to perform at Senior. The gap is usually not technical — it is about what you default to when no one is watching. Work through these questions honestly.

3. Consistency check — mark each behaviour honestly

Choose Consistently only if you do this reliably, without reminders, across different projects and pressures — not just on your best days.

Part 3 Growth Focus Areas Pick 2–3 dimensions to focus on over the next 6–12 months

These don't have to be your lowest scores. Choose areas that are most energising, most aligned to where you want to go, or most relevant to the work ahead.

Part 4 Consistency Commitments Turn your gaps into 3–5 concrete behavioural commitments

Growth at this level is not won through single impressive moments. It is built through consistent, repeated behaviour across many ordinary situations. Choose 3–5 behaviours from your consistency gaps or focus areas and commit to them.

What consistency actually means:

Do it even when inconvenient. Do it without being reminded. Do it on the boring work, not just the projects you find interesting. A behaviour performed consistently for three months is often not yet visible to others as "the way you work" — give it six months.

Review at 30 days: am I actually doing this? — 60 days: is this becoming a default? — 90 days: would someone who hasn't seen this exercise notice a change?

Part 5 OKRs Turn your focus areas into measurable objectives

One or two well-crafted OKRs are more useful than three vague ones. Key Results should describe outcomes, not tasks.
